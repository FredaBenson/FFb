All About Sharks

Many sharks are very unique

Hammerhead Shark

This shark's unusual name comes from the unusual shape of its head, an amazing piece of anatomy built to maximize the fish's ability to find its favorite meal: stingrays.

A hammerhead shark uses its wide head to trap stingrays by pinning them to the seafloor.

The shark's eye placement, on each end of its very wide head, allows it to scan more area more quickly than other sharks can.

The hammerhead also has special sensors across its head that helps it scan for food in the ocean.

Great White Shark

Great white sharks are torpedo-shaped with powerful tails that can propel them through the water at up to 35 miles per hour.

The great white shark is a type of mackerel shark from the Lamnidae family, which also includes mako sharks, salmon sharks, and porbeagle sharks.

Great whites are the largest of the bunch—and the world’s largest predatory fish.

Great whites are named after their white underbellies, but their topsides can come in a variety of browns and grays.

Whale Shark

Whale sharks are the largest fish in the world, reaching up to 40 feet (12 meters) long and 20.6 tons (18.7 metric tonnes) in weight.

Despite their massive size, they are gentle giants that feed on tiny plankton, small fish, and crustaceans, using their specialized filtering apparatus in their mouth.

They have over 3,000 tiny teeth, but they don't use them for feeding. They also have teeth-like structures on their eyeballs, probably to protect them from the harsh ocean environment.

They belong to the group of fish that have skeletons made of cartilage, not bone, like sharks, rays, and skates. Their scientific name is Rhincodon typus, meaning "rasp-toothed fish".

They are migratory and can be found in warm waters around the world, from Australia to Mexico. They often gather in large groups for feeding or mating, sometimes forming aggregations of hundreds of individuals.

hey are endangered due to overfishing, habitat loss, and collisions with boats. They can live up to 100 years, but their exact lifespan and reproduction are still poorly understood

Wobbegong Shark

Wobbegong sharks are not your typical sharks. They look like lumpy carpets with ornate patterns and shaggy beards.

These sharks belong to the family Orectolobidae, which has 12 species of wobbegongs in three genera. They are mostly found in Australia and Indonesia, except for the Japanese wobbegong.

Wobbegongs are bottom-dwellers, preferring to rest on rocks and coral reefs. They use their camouflage to blend in with the ocean floor and ambush their prey.

Wobbegongs are nocturnal hunters, feeding on fish, crustaceans, and sometimes other sharks. They have powerful jaws and sharp teeth that can inflict serious wounds.

Wobbegongs are usually not aggressive to humans, unless provoked or disturbed. They can bite and hold on to divers or snorkelers who get too close or step on them.

Wobbegongs are fascinating and unique sharks that deserve respect and conservation. They are vulnerable to habitat loss, fishing, and pollution.

Nurse Shark

Nurse sharks adapt well to captivity and are an important species for research, primarily in the area of shark physiology.

The species is fished for food and leather.

Because of their docile nature, nurse sharks are popular with divers and ecotourists.

Nurse sharks occur in warm tropical and subtropical waters off coasts of the Eastern and Western Atlantic and the Eastern Pacific.

Nurse sharks have a low metabolismand generally expend minimal energy.

While most sharks need to move to breathe, nurse sharks can rest motionless on the sea floor.

Bull Shark

Bull sharks get their name from their short, blunt snout, as well as their pugnacious disposition and a tendency to head-butt their prey before attacking.

They are medium-size sharks, with thick, stout bodies and long pectoral fins.

They are found cruising the shallow, warm waters of all the world’s oceans.

Fast, agile predators, they will eat almost anything they see, including fish, dolphins, and even other sharks.

Humans are not, per se, on their menus.

Sand Tiger Shark

Sand tiger sharks are brownish-gray with rust-colored spots on top and white underneath.

Their name comes from their tendency toward shoreline habitats, and they are often seen trolling the ocean floor in the surf zone, very close to shore.

Sand tigers are the only shark known to come to the surface and gulp air.

Sand tiger sharks are denser than water and maintain buoyancy by swallowing air and holding it in their stomachs.

Leopard Shark

They are a type of hound shark with a banded pattern on their back and dark spots on their sides.

They have teeth with three points and a dorsal fin at the midpoint of their body.

They can grow up to 6 feet long and weigh up to 40 pounds.

They live in North America, from Oregon to Mexico, and are commonly seen in or near the surf zone.

They are not dangerous to humans and feed on crabs, clams, shrimp, fish eggs, larger fish, other small sharks, and octopuses.

Tresher Shark

They are not dangerous to humans and feed on crabs, clams, shrimp, fish eggs, larger fish, other small sharks, and octopuses.

They mature when they reach about 5 years old and 5 to 9 feet in length, and mate in midsummer.

They are ovoviviparous, meaning that eggs develop inside the female's body, but the young are not attached by a placenta.

They eat schooling fish, cephalopods, and sometimes crabs and shrimp, and adjust with whatever is easily available.

They have a torpedo-shaped trunk, a short, broad head, a pointed, conical snout, and moderately large eyes.

Megamouth Shark

They are enormous sharks, with males being 13 ft long and females being 16 ft long.

They have a wide mouth that can open up to 4 ft, but a small and round snout and tiny teeth.

They live in coastal to open ocean habitats, in tropical to temperate latitudes worldwide.

Their lifespan is unknown, but they are estimated to live up to 100 years.

They weigh up to 2,700 pounds (1,215 kg).

Horn Shark

Sharks portal The horn shark (Heterodontus francisci) is a species of bullhead shark, in the family Heterodontidae.

It is endemic to the coastal waters off the western coast of North America, from California to the Gulf of California.

The horn shark is a sporadic swimmer that prefers to use its flexible, muscular pectoral fins to push itself along the bottom.

Under normal circumstances, horn sharks are harmless to humans and can readily be approached underwater.

Horn sharks are a type of ground shark, with a heavy body and a broad, flattened head.

The Horn Shark is a small, common, bottom-dweller in the warm waters off western North America.

Porbeagle Shark

It can grow up to 12 feet long and weigh as much as 600 pounds.

It has a conical snout, white underbelly, dark gray top, and large black eyes.

It must remain in constant motion to breathe properly.

It migrates long distances, sometimes more than 1,000 miles, to reach feeding or mating grounds.

It has been seen jumping out of the water in pursuit of prey.

Blacktip Reef Shark

Blacktip reef sharks are small but fierce predators that patrol the coral reefs of the Indo-Pacific region, hunting fish, crustaceans and cephalopods.

Unlike most sharks, they are social animals that form groups of up to 10 individuals. They cooperate to chase and corner their prey, and sometimes perform acrobatic leaps out of the water.

They are viviparous, meaning they give birth to live young, usually four pups at a time. The pups stay in shallow nurseries for protection, and mature around four to seven years old.

They are curious and inquisitive when they encounter divers, but usually shy away if threatened. However, they can become aggressive when there is food involved, especially speared fish or bait.

They are among the most common and resilient sharks in their habitat, but they face threats from overfishing, habitat degradation and climate change. They are considered near threatened by the IUCN.

Shortfin Mako Shark

The shortfin mako shark is a large and powerful mackerel shark that can reach speeds of up to 70 km/h, making it the fastest swimming shark in the world.

It has a sleek, torpedo-shaped body, a pointed snout, and long, narrow teeth that protrude from its mouth. Its color ranges from metallic blue to gray on the back and white on the belly.

It is found in tropical and temperate seas, usually in open waters and near the surface. It can also dive to depths of over 500 m and tolerate low temperatures.

It is an active and agile predator that feeds on fish, squid, octopus, sea turtles, dolphins, and other sharks. It can leap up to 6 m above the water, often when hooked or attacked.

It is a solitary and nomadic shark that migrates long distances across the oceans. It rarely interacts with other sharks, except during mating season, which occurs every two to three years.

It is a slow-growing and late-maturing shark that faces threats from overfishing, bycatch, and habitat degradation. It is classified as endangered by the IUCN and needs urgent conservation measures.

Silky Shark

The silky shark is one of the three most common pelagic sharks along with the blue and oceanic whitetip sharks, and counts among the most numerous large oceanic animals in the world with a population of at least tens of millions.

The Silky Shark, also known as the Carcharhinus Falciformis, gets its name from the smooth and silky texture of its skin.

The Silky Shark (Carcharhinus falciformis), is a large pelagic (found in open waters) shark of tropical and warm temperate seas.

Silky sharks are smooth, sleek oceanic predators with densely packed dermal denticles that give their skin a distinct sheen.

The Silky shark (Carcharhinus falciformis) is a type of shark of the genus Carcharhinus, family Carcharhinidae.

Blue Shark

They are slender sharks with a long snout, blue coloring on top, and white underside.

They are pelagic sharks, meaning they live in the open ocean, and can be found worldwide in deep water of tropic and temperate oceans.

They are carnivorous and eat squid, shrimp, lobster, sea birds, and smaller sharks.

They have a keen sense of electromagnetism and can detect a heart beating from miles away.

They are viviparous, meaning they give birth to live young, and can have huge litter sizes of up to 100 pups.

Oceanic Whitetip Shark

Oceanic whitetip shark is a large, pelagic shark with distinctive white-tipped fins.

It inhabits tropical and warm temperate seas, usually in the upper water column near the surface.

It feeds on a variety of prey, including bony fish, cephalopods, sea birds, marine mammals and even garbage.

It is considered one of the most dangerous sharks to humans, especially to shipwreck and plane crash survivors.

It is critically endangered due to overfishing for its fins, meat, skin and oil, as well as bycatch in tuna fisheries.

It has a slow reproduction rate, with females maturing at 6-9 years and giving birth to 1-15 live young.

Basking Shark

The basking shark is the second-largest living shark and fish, after the whale shark, and one of three plankton-eating shark species, along with the whale shark and megamouth shark.

The basking shark has an enormous liver that runs over much of their body length and accounts for up to 25% of their body weight. They use it to help it with buoyancy.

The basking shark got its name because they swim slowly and methodically near the surface as they feed.

Basking sharks aren’t afraid of boats or people and will swim close to divers.

Usually solitary, males and females come together to mate during the summer.

During the 20th century, basking sharks were widely fished for their liver oil—used in lamps, cosmetics, perfumes, and lubricants—as well as their meat, skin, and large fins.

Brown Banded Bamboo Shark

The brownbanded bamboo shark (Chiloscyllium punctatum), is a bamboo shark in the family Hemiscylliidae that can be found in the Indo-West Pacific from Japan to northern Australia, between latitudes 34° N and 26° S.

These sharks can be distinguished by their concave posterior margined dorsal fin. No color patterns are present for the adults, however, the juveniles have dark transverse bands with some dark spots.

These sharks live around coral reefs and tide pools.

The minimum size of the tank that can carry an adult cat shark is about 180 gallons.

These sharks are carnivores that should be fed 2-3 times a week; some of their diet includes fresh shrimp, scallop, squid, and marine fishes.

Its minimum population doubling is in the 4.5–14 years range.

Tiger Shark

The tiger shark is a solitary, mostly nocturnal hunter.

It is notable for having the widest food spectrum of all sharks, with a range of prey that includes crustaceans, fish, seals, birds, squid, turtles, sea snakes, dolphins, and even other, smaller sharks.

The tiger shark (Galeocerdo cuvier) is a species of ground shark, and the only extant member of the genus Galeocerdo and family Galeocerdonidae.

The shark was first described by Peron and Lesueur in 1822, and was given the name Squalus cuvier.

The tiger shark commonly attains adult length of 3.5 to 4.7 m (11 ft 6 in – 15 ft 5 in) and weighs between 300 and 900 kg (700 and 2,000 lb).

The tiger shark is often found close to the coast, mainly in tropical and subtropical waters throughout the world.

Goblin Shark

Goblin sharks are rare, deep-sea predators with a distinctive snout and protruding jaws.

They belong to the Mitsukurinidae family, which dates back to the Cretaceous period, over 100 million years ago.

They have pinkish skin that lacks pigment and reveals their blood vessels underneath. Their skin color changes after death.

They can sense the electric fields of their prey, such as fish, squid, and crustaceans, with their long, sensitive snouts.

They can extend their elastic jaws out of their mouths to snatch their prey, using sharp, nail-like teeth.

They are mostly found off the coast of Japan, but have been caught in various parts of the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans.
